About Me

Summary

Johntrell Bowles is a public health and social policy professional with deep expertise in Medicaid, disability benefits, and social insurance programs. Currently completing an MPH at Yale University, he brings a strong analytical foundation in both qualitative and quantitative research, including policy evaluation and program design. Johntrell has contributed to federal and state-level health policy through roles at MACPAC, Mercer, and the Social Security Administration, where he worked across Medicaid HCBS, SSI/SSDI eligibility, and integrated care for dual eligibles. His work is grounded in a commitment to health equity and economic justice, as demonstrated through his thesis on the political and cultural resilience of the SSI program and his leadership in fellowships such as the John R. Lewis and Public Policy New Voices programs. With a background in client-facing consulting, government service, and academic research, Johntrell offers a rare blend of policy insight, lived community engagement, and systems-level strategy.

Research Interests

Public Health Policy

Focus on the resilience and evolution of social safety net programs amid changing political and economic climates.

Disability Policy and Economic Security

Analyze how federal programs like SSI and SSDI promote or hinder economic self-sufficiency for disabled individuals, with attention to benefit adequacy, eligibility, and systemic exclusion.

Medicaid and Long-Term Services and Supports (LTSS)

Examine state-level innovation and disparities in access to Medicaid HCBS and LTSS, with an emphasis on waiver design, fast-tracking strategies, and equity in non-MAGI populations.

Stigma and Welfare Participation

Explore how narratives of deservingness, media framing, and administrative burdens deter eligible populations from participating in critical safety net programs.

Integrated Care for Dually Eligible Beneficiaries

Investigate the effectiveness of integrated Medicare-Medicaid models in improving outcomes and care coordination for dual eligibles.

Policy Implementation and Bureaucratic Design

Study how frontline bureaucracies and administrative systems shape public access to services, with a focus on public trust, transparency, and institutional accountability.

Social Policy

Study the design, evolution, and political sustainability of U.S. social welfare programs, with particular emphasis on how institutional structures and cultural narratives shape program participation, reform, and equity.

Long-Term Care Policy

Analyze Medicaid-funded LTSS and Home- and Community-Based Services (HCBS) programs, with focus on waiver design, access disparities for non-MAGI populations, and strategies for promoting aging in place and community integration.

Medicaid and Medicare Policy

Examine how states structure and implement Medicaid and Medicare policies to serve dual eligibles and vulnerable populations, with emphasis on integrated care models, administrative barriers, and federal-state interactions.

Welfare Economics

Explore the economic rationale, design, and trade-offs of cash assistance and social insurance programs, particularly SSI and SSDI, through the lenses of behavioral economics, poverty alleviation, and incentive structures.

Income Support and Social Safety Nets

Investigate the role of means-tested and universal benefit programs in reducing poverty and inequality, with attention to stigma, administrative burdens, and the political legitimacy of different forms of assistance.

State Health Policy

Evaluate how state-level political, fiscal, and institutional factors shape public health and healthcare delivery systems, including Medicaid innovation waivers, managed care, and access to long-term services and supports.

Tax Policy

Examine the intersection of taxation and social equity, with a focus on how federal and state tax structures affect income distribution, public program funding, and the economic security of low-income and disabled populations.

Supplemental Security Income (SSI) Policy

Analyze the historical development, political neglect, and structural design of the SSI program, with particular emphasis on benefit adequacy, administrative burdens, marriage penalties, and the role of stigma in shaping program participation and reform efforts.

Aging and Economic Security

Investigate how aging-related public programs—such as SSI, Medicaid LTSS, and Social Security—support or fail older adults, with focus on income adequacy, long-term care access, intergenerational equity, and the structural drivers of aging inequities.
